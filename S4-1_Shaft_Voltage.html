Shaft Voltage — Causes and Mechanism
Section 4 — Bearing Electric Stress
Shaft Voltage Capacitive Divider Model
Root Cause
The inverter's switching produces a common-mode voltage (VCM) on the motor output — the average of the three-phase voltages. This voltage drives current through parasitic capacitances between the winding and rotor, appearing as a shaft-to-ground voltage.
Capacitive Voltage Divider
Shaft voltage is a fraction of VCM, determined by the ratio of winding-to-rotor capacitance (Cwr) to rotor-to-bearing capacitance (Crb). This ratio is fixed by motor design — the user cannot change it. Typical shaft-voltage ratio: 5–25% of VCM.
High-Frequency Circulating Current
In motors > 100 kW with long rotors, a circulating bearing current mechanism also occurs: the changing magnetic flux creates a loop current through stator frame → bearing → shaft → opposite bearing. This is distinct from EDM but occurs in the same motors.
When It Becomes Failure
When Vshaft exceeds the bearing lubricant breakdown voltage (~10 V), a spark discharge crosses the film. Each discharge removes a microscopic volume of metal from the race surface. Millions of discharges per hour produce the characteristic frosted (fluted) surface — leading to rough running and eventual spalling.
Key Concept
Shaft voltage is caused by capacitive coupling between the inverter's CM voltage and the rotor — it cannot be eliminated by the motor designer. The only effective controls are shaft grounding brushes (provide a low-impedance discharge path) or CM output filters (reduce VCM at the motor terminals).
