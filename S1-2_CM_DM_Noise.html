Common-Mode and Differential-Mode Noise
Section 1 — What Is EMC?
Noise Current Paths — Differential-Mode vs. Common-Mode
Differential-Mode
Noise current flows in opposite directions on the two conductors of a circuit. It behaves like a signal and appears in series with the useful voltage. Filtered by series inductors and capacitors across the line.
Common-Mode
Noise current flows in the same direction on all conductors. Its return path is via the ground/PE conductor or equipment chassis. This is the dominant mode in AC drive systems — driven by parasitic capacitance to motor frame.
Why CM Is the Problem
CM current from a drive returns through the PE system. Without a local, low-impedance return path (shielded cable), it flows through the mains supply — causing interference in other equipment connected to the same supply.
RFI vs. EMC
RFI (Radio Frequency Interference) is an older, narrower term for interference with radio reception. EMC is the modern, broader standard covering all electrical equipment. 46 CFR §143.575 uses the term "radio reception interference" — meaning RFI control is a regulatory requirement on vessels.
Key Concept
AC drives produce primarily common-mode noise — current that flows on all output conductors simultaneously with its return path via ground. The correct solution is a low-impedance ground return path provided by a shielded motor cable with 360° terminations at both ends.
