Four Coupling Mechanisms
Section 1 — What Is EMC?
⚡
1
Galvanic Coupling — Direct Conductive Connection
A direct wire or metallic path is shared between the noise source and the victim circuit. Noise current flows through the common impedance. Example: shared neutral conductor or common PE rail.
▮
2
Capacitive Coupling — Through Parasitic Capacitances
High-frequency noise transfers between conductors through parasitic capacitances — the stray capacitance between motor windings and frame is the primary capacitive path in AC drive systems. Proportional to dV/dt.
⇋
3
Inductive Coupling — Magnetic Field Between Conductors
A changing current in one conductor induces a voltage in a nearby conductor via mutual inductance. Minimised by separating power and signal cables and by using twisted pairs for signal wiring.
∼
4
Electromagnetic Coupling — Radiation and Reception
Energy radiates from a conductor acting as an antenna. A second conductor picks up the electromagnetic wave. Dominant at high frequencies (> 30 MHz). Shielded cables break this path.
Source → Path → Victim Model
Every EMI problem has three elements: a source generating the disturbance, a coupling path (one of the four above), and a victim circuit. Removing any one of the three eliminates the problem.
Dominant Paths in AC Drive Installations
Capacitive coupling dominates at drive output frequencies — parasitic capacitance between winding and motor frame drives common-mode current. Inductive coupling between parallel motor and control cables causes differential interference on signal circuits.
⚒ Marine Context — Life-Safety Receivers
On a towboat, AIS transponders and VHF radios are life-safety devices. 46 CFR §143.575(5) requires that cable routing avoids interference with radio reception and compass. Electromagnetic coupling from improperly screened drive cables is a real risk.
Key Concept
Identifying which coupling mechanism is active is the first step in fixing any EMI problem. In AC drive systems, capacitive coupling via parasitic motor-frame capacitance is the dominant mechanism — producing common-mode current that must be controlled through correct grounding and shielding.
