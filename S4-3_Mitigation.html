EDM Mitigation Strategy
Section 4 — Bearing Electric Stress
1
Verify Shaft Alignment
Before applying electrical mitigation, confirm the mechanical condition is sound. Laser-align the drive train to within ±0.05 mm angular and parallel offsets. Misalignment causes bearing overload that makes EDM damage worse and faster.
2
Verify Lubrication
Confirm correct grease type and quantity. Over-greasing is as damaging as under-greasing — it churns, heats, and degrades rapidly. For e-frac mobile equipment: confirm grease is compatible with expected temperature range (−40 °C to +70 °C).
3
Install Shaft Grounding Brush (Drive End)
A carbon-fibre or silver-graphite brush provides a continuous low-impedance conductive path from shaft to motor frame, bypassing the bearing film. The brush continuously drains the shaft voltage — prevents charge build-up that causes sparking. Marine: also prevents stray current corrosion.
4
Install Insulated Bearing (Non-Drive End)
For large motors (> 100 kW) where circulating bearing current is a risk: fit an insulated bearing at the non-drive end (NDE). The insulated outer race breaks the circulating current loop through the motor frame. Never insulate both ends — the shaft must have one path to ground.
5
Install CM Output Filter Cores (if > 75 kW)
For high-power motors: nanocrystalline CM filter cores on the motor cable reduce VCM at the motor terminals. This reduces shaft voltage at source — complementing the shaft grounding brush. All three phase conductors must pass through the core (Section 3 rule).
Marine Propulsion Specifics
Shaft grounding brushes on the propeller shaft are standard marine engineering practice — they were used for corrosion protection long before VSD-driven propulsion was common.
Under 46 CFR §143.570(f), metallic towing vessels must not return CM current to the distribution system through the hull. Shaft brushes address both the bearing EDM risk and this regulatory requirement simultaneously.
Single-fault tolerance (§143.585): The propulsion shaft grounding system should be designed so that loss of a single brush does not immediately result in propulsion loss — use redundant brushes on large drives.
E-Frac Mobile Equipment
E-frac pump motors operate at high load factors in continuous duty — bearing temperatures are elevated, making EDM-induced lubrication breakdown faster than in intermittent-duty motors.
Trailer-mounted equipment sees road vibration during transit and pumping vibration in service — check shaft alignment after every relocation, not just at installation.
CM filter cores on long wellsite cable runs (> 50 m from VFD to motor) reduce both motor winding stress (Section 3) and bearing CM current — a dual benefit.
