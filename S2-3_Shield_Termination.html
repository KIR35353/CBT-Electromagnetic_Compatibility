Correct Shield Termination
Section 2 — Grounding & Shielding
✓
360° EMC Cable Gland — Drive End
A metallic cable gland with a circumferential clamping ring makes contact around the full 360° of the cable braid. The gland is threaded into the drive's cable entry plate, which is bolted directly to the backplate. Low inductance, good at MHz frequencies.
CORRECT
✓
360° EMC Cable Gland — Motor End
The same gland type at the motor terminal box. The motor frame must be earthed (bonded to PE). Both-end termination creates a Faraday cage around the cable core conductors, fully containing the CM current inside the shield.
CORRECT
i
Shield Continuity and Ground Loop Prevention
At power-frequency, a cable shield bonded at both ends creates a ground loop that can carry 50/60 Hz circulating current. This is acceptable in drive installations — the CM noise benefits outweigh the minor power-frequency current in the shield. No ferrite is needed.
ACCEPTABLE
✗
Pigtail Termination — Drive or Motor End
The braid is folded back and twisted into a single wire connected to a terminal. The pigtail inductance is ~10 nH. At 10 MHz this represents >600 mΩ of impedance — 100× worse than a 360° gland — making the shield almost useless above 1 MHz.
INCORRECT
✗
Single-End Termination (Drive End Only)
Grounding the shield at only one end prevents 50 Hz ground loop current — but it means CM current has no return path at the motor end. The shield is only ~20% effective. This practice is sometimes recommended for signal cables but is wrong for motor cables.
INCORRECT
Why Both Ends?
For CM current to flow from the motor frame back to the drive chassis via the shield, the shield must form a closed loop. Grounding one end creates an open circuit for the return current — forcing it to find another path (mains supply or vessel structure).
EMC Gland vs. Terminal
A 360° EMC cable gland achieves < 5 mΩ contact impedance at the cable entry. A pigtail to a terminal achieves 600–6000 mΩ at 10 MHz. This single difference explains most site EMC failures that occur despite using "shielded" cable.
⚒ Marine Requirement
46 CFR §143.575(5) requires vessel wiring to be installed and routed to avoid interference with radio reception and compass. Correct 360° shield termination is the primary technical control for compliance with this requirement on AC drive installations.
Key Concept
The cable shield must be terminated with 360° EMC cable glands at both ends — at the drive cable entry plate and at the motor terminal box. A pigtail or single-end termination reduces shielding effectiveness by one to two orders of magnitude at the frequencies that matter.
