Standards Hierarchy — Basic, Generic, Product
Section 5 — Standards & Regulations
▼ Higher specificity — product standard takes precedence ▼
1
Basic Standards — Test Methods Only
EN 55011 / CISPR 11  |  EN 61000-4 series  |  IEC 61000-4-2 to -6
Define how to measure — the test methods, equipment, and setups for conducted emissions, radiated emissions, ESD, surge, burst, etc. They do not specify any emission or immunity limits. Used by all subsequent standards as a reference.
Test methods No limits Measurement setups
2
Generic Standards — Environment-Based Limits
EN 61000-6-4 (Industrial emission)  |  EN 61000-6-2 (Industrial immunity)
EN 61000-6-3 (Residential emission)  |  EN 61000-6-1 (Residential immunity)
Apply when no product standard exists for a specific equipment type. Specify limits based on the environment (residential vs. industrial). For AC drives: a product standard does exist (IEC 61800-3) — so the generic standard is superseded and should not be used for drives.
Environment-based limits Residential / Industrial Fallback when no product std
3
Product Standards — Specific Equipment Limits
EN/IEC 61800-3 (AC drives)  |  IEC 60533 (Marine vessels)  |  46 CFR §143.570/575 (US vessels)
Product standards take precedence over generic standards. They account for the specific characteristics of the product type and define tighter or different limits appropriate for that equipment. EN/IEC 61800-3 is the product standard for power drive systems. When it applies, the generic standard is set aside.
Highest precedence Product-specific limits EN/IEC 61800-3 for drives IEC 60533 / 46 CFR for marine
Precedence rule: Product standard > Generic standard > Basic standard. When a product standard exists for your equipment type — use it. Using a generic standard when a product standard applies is a common compliance error.
Why the Hierarchy Matters for Drives
If you test a VSD installation to EN 61000-6-4 (generic industrial) rather than EN/IEC 61800-3, you may pass limits that are looser in some frequency ranges and miss the more relevant drive-specific requirements. The CE mark requires the correct standard to be cited.
For US vessels and e-frac: the CE-mark standard chain (Directive → EN/IEC 61800-3) does not apply. The installer must comply with 46 CFR Part 143 and, for marine, IEC 60533. However, understanding EN/IEC 61800-3 C-categories is still essential for interpreting drive datasheets.
The installer's responsibility: the drive manufacturer declares a category (e.g., C2 without external filter, C1 with filter). The installer must ensure the complete PDS (drive + cable + motor) is installed according to the declared category. Deviating from the category (e.g., longer cable than declared) means the CE mark no longer covers the installation.
Marine Standard IEC 60533 Overview
IEC 60533:2015 — EMC requirements for ships, offshore structures, and marine units. Specifies emission and immunity limits for all shipboard electrical equipment including VSD propulsion systems.
Test environments: Class A (below decks, machinery spaces) and Class B (navigation bridge, sensitive equipment spaces). Drive and motor installations are Class A equipment.
IEC 60533 references the same basic standards (IEC 61000-4 series) for immunity tests. Limits are calibrated for the ship environment — typically more demanding in the 2–30 MHz range (HF communication systems are critical for marine safety).
